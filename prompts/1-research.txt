CORE CHANNEL CONTEXT
Protect Our Plates creates consumer-watchdog videos about food and drink products sold in the United Kingdom, especially products commonly found in UK supermarkets.
The videos normally:
Investigate well-known food or drink products.
Identify products viewers may want to avoid.
Give convincing and varied reasons for concern.
Contrast advertising, packaging, and reputation with the available evidence.
Compare older and modern versions where evidence permits.
Discuss recipe changes, ingredient substitutions, reduced sizes, quality changes, value, marketing, public disappointment, and other relevant concerns.
Recommend a smaller number of products that may still be worth buying.
Use strong but responsible language.
Reflect the real thoughts and frustrations of the British public.
Combine consumer protection, nostalgia, outrage, practical information, and useful recommendations.
The primary target audience is:
Mostly aged fifty and above.
Primarily British.
Familiar with major UK supermarkets.
Familiar with long-established food brands.
Often nostalgic about older recipes, ingredients, sizes, packaging, flavours, and product standards.
Often sceptical about major corporations, reformulations, shrinkflation, cost-cutting, lower-quality substitutes, and declining food standards.
Likely to appreciate plain-spoken, traditional, patriotic, consumer-first messaging.
Interested in hearing observations that reflect what ordinary members of the British public already think and say.
Research should therefore focus heavily on products that are popular, familiar, recognised, or emotionally relevant to British viewers aged fifty and above.

6. Preserve existing lists when one appears

7. Legal and reputational safety
The video should be shocking, click-worthy, and strongly argued, but it must not make unsupported accusations or create avoidable cease-and-desist risk.
Flag claims that:
Assign dishonest motives without evidence.
Accuse a company of deliberately harming consumers.
Rely only on anonymous comments.

Confuse correlation with causation.
Use outdated ingredient lists.

Describe ordinary processing as inherently harmful without support.
Use words such as scam, criminal, dishonest, deceptive, poisoning, or fraud without authoritative evidence.
Recommend protective wording when necessary, including:

“The current ingredient list shows…”
“At the time of writing…”
“Reportedly…”
“Consumers have complained that…”
“Some long-time buyers say…”
“The exact details are unclear, but…”
“The available evidence suggests…”
“The company says…”
“The product appears to have changed…”
Never recommend using the word “fraud” in the final script.
