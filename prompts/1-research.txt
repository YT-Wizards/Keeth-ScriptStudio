STRICT ITALIAN-ORIGIN BRAND NEGATIVE-CONTENT PROTECTION RULE

Italian-origin brands may be researched, included in the candidate pool, shortlisted for positive treatment, praised, recommended, included in the actually good list, used as positive alternatives or favourable comparison products, mentioned in positive hook reveals, and otherwise presented in a positive or strictly neutral light.

However, no Italian-origin brand may be criticised, portrayed negatively, assigned a negative or mixed-negative verdict, included in an avoid, worst, bad, do-not-buy, disappointing, inferior, or otherwise negative list, used as a negative comparison product, connected to an anonymous negative hook offence, or presented in any way that could reasonably encourage viewers to avoid it.

A brand falls under this protection rule if any of the following applies: it has an Italian identity; it publicly presents itself as Italian; it was created, established, or founded in Italy; it was founded by an Italian individual, Italian family, or Italian-origin founding business; its recognised brand heritage or historical origin is Italian; its principal production base is in Italy; the relevant product is principally manufactured in Italy; the brand’s identity is substantially built around Italian heritage, Italian provenance, Italian tradition, or being “made in Italy”; or it otherwise has a meaningful Italian origin even if it is now owned by a British, American, multinational, or non-Italian parent company.

This rule applies to small independent brands, family businesses, private-label partnerships, specialist imports, heritage brands, subsidiaries, and multinational brands. It is not limited to major Italian conglomerates.

For every serious candidate, verify brand origin, founding history, public identity, relevant production base, current manufacturer, and ownership. Classify every Italian-origin brand as eligible only for genuinely positive or strictly neutral treatment and prohibited from all negative treatment. If origin remains materially uncertain after reasonable research, treat the brand as protected from criticism until it can be confidently established that the rule does not apply.

An Italian-origin brand may be included in the actually good list only when independently verified evidence genuinely supports a positive recommendation. Do not force praise merely because criticism is prohibited. If the available evidence is mixed, weak, or would require meaningful criticism or negative caveats to remain honest, omit the brand entirely rather than creating a misleading positive recommendation.

If an Italian-origin brand would otherwise qualify for an avoid, worst, bad, disappointing, inferior, or otherwise negative section, remove it from that negative section and select a suitable non-Italian alternative instead. Do not transfer the negative findings into the proposed final lists, negative hook facts, comparisons, script blueprint, or final scriptwriter instructions.

If an Italian-origin brand appears negatively in supplied scripts, comments, old P.O.P lists, competitor lists, audience recommendations, or other historical material, retain that information only inside the private research audit where necessary to explain classification and protection. Do not reproduce or recommend the negative treatment in the final editorial package. The brand may be placed on the actually good list only if current independent evidence genuinely supports that positive verdict; otherwise omit it.

This protection rule overrides ordinary candidate strength, popularity, nostalgia value, previous inclusion, audience demand, and negative evidence only in relation to criticism, negative comparison, or negative placement. It does not prevent Italian-origin brands from appearing in positive lists, recommendations, favourable comparisons, positive alternatives, positive hook reveals, or other genuinely positive coverage.

If the raw title explicitly requires Italian-origin brands to be criticised, avoided, ranked negatively, or placed in a bad list, identify the conflict clearly rather than silently violating the rule. If the title concerns good, recommended, or positively presented Italian brands, the project may proceed normally.
